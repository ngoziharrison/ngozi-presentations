Intersectionality is a term coined by Kimberlé Crenshaw and describes approaches understanding social phenomena and systems by centering interlocking systems of oppression to make visible normative value systems that valorize heteronormativity, patriarchy, whiteness, etc. Intersectionality was originally developed within legal studies and has been leveraged as a wide variety of disciplines

12

Another important critical scholar is Patricia Hill Collins who builds on the concept of intersectionality and introduces the matrix of domination. Matrix thinking provides a paradigm for us to understand the many dimensions of of how one might experience domination or oppression, particularly focused on Black women

Potemkin Village is a term used to refer to a fake external facade that attempts to make a situation look better or flashier then it truly is.

The term comes from the story of fake villages made by Grigory Potemkin, a field marshal of the Russian Empire in the 18th century, to impress Catherine the Great when she visited Crimea after its annexation from the Ottoman Empire

Why focus on labor when discussing technology?

  • Often, when we talk about technology, we focus on the consumer side; however, this can cause us to miss the more opaque and mundane ways in which technology operates
  • Workers’ lives are increasingly controlled by algorithms that are used by corporations to maximize efficiency
  • Focusing on labor when discussing technology reveals the often hidden ways that technology and those in power control society and use technology for domination. We can also find ways of resisting or using technology for justice and liberation
  • Alongside transformations in information and communication technologies (ICTs) and revolutions in industrialization, labor practices have changed. Our goal is to study these changes.

BEAM/BEAT Framework

The BEAM/BEAT framework developed by Joseph Bizup provides a way of thinking about sources in relation to your writing and research

36

Background Sources

These sources provide information that sets up an argument. Background sources provide important context, set up stakes, or provide the logical grounding for your claims

37

Exhibit Sources

Exhibit sources provide evidence and material for analysis or interpretation

38

Argument Sources

Argument sources provide claims that you as the writer will engage with in some way. You may agree and use them to support your argument or diverge from or challenge these claims

39

Theory/Method Sources

Theory sources provide concepts, theoretical frameworks, or vocabulary that will inform your approach

Method sources provide the method that you will use in your analysis or build on

40

Often a source can be used in any of these four ways
